# Round 1: Recruiter Screen — How to Pass It Excellently

> **Format**: Phone or video call | **Duration**: 30 min | **Interviewer**: Dedicated recruiter (Big Tech has full-time recruiting teams assigned to specific orgs)

---

## What This Round Actually Is

The recruiter screen at Big Tech is **not a casual chat** — it is a structured **qualification, calibration, and logistics gate**. Big Tech recruiters handle hundreds of candidates simultaneously and make fast, data-driven decisions. Roughly **40 – 60% of candidates are screened out here**.

The recruiter is evaluating five things in 30 minutes:

| # | What They're Checking | Why It Matters |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Communication clarity** | If you can't articulate your background in 60 seconds, you won't survive a 5-round loop. |
| 2 | **Qualification match** | Do your years of experience, skills, and domain match the role and level? |
| 3 | **Level calibration** | Are you an L3? L5? L6? The level determines which interview loop you enter — and the bar for every subsequent round. |
| 4 | **Compensation alignment** | Big Tech has well-defined bands. If you want $500K and the band tops at $350K, this ends early. |
| 5 | **Logistics & timeline** | Visa status, notice period, competing offers, location preferences, remote/hybrid expectations. |

> **Critical insight**: At Big Tech, the recruiter is your **internal advocate**. If you impress them, they will coach you on what to expect, share internal prep resources, and push the hiring manager to prioritize your loop. Treat the recruiter as an ally, not an obstacle.

## Before the Call (60 – 90 min of prep)

### 1. Research the Company AND the Specific Team

Big Tech companies have **dozens of AI teams**. "I'm excited about Google" means nothing. "I'm excited about the Gemini inference team's work on efficient multi-modal serving" means everything.

**Company-level research** (15 min):
- The company's current AI strategy and flagship products.
- Recent AI product launches or announcements (last 3 – 6 months).
- Any public controversy or challenges (layoffs, regulatory issues, product failures) — you should be aware, even if you don't bring it up.

**Team-level research** (30 min):
- What does THIS specific team build? (Reading the JD carefully usually tells you.)
- What engineering blog posts has this org published? Check:
  - Google AI Blog / DeepMind Blog
  - Meta AI Blog / FAIR Research
  - Amazon Science
  - Microsoft Research Blog
  - OpenAI Blog / Anthropic Research
  - NVIDIA Developer Blog
- Who is on the team? (Search LinkedIn for "[Company] [Team Name]" — look at people's titles, backgrounds, and recent posts.)
- What open-source projects or papers has this team published?

**Industry context** (15 min):
- What are the competitive dynamics? (If interviewing at Google Gemini, know what OpenAI, Anthropic, and Meta are doing.)
- What's the current state of the AI space relevant to this team's work?

### 2. Know Your Compensation Numbers Cold

Big Tech compensation has **four components**. You need to understand all of them:

| Component | What It Is | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Base salary** | Annual cash salary | Usually the smallest differentiator between companies |
| **RSU / Stock** | Restricted Stock Units vesting over 4 years (various schedules) | Often the largest component at senior levels. Google: front-loaded. Amazon: back-loaded (5/15/40/40). Meta: evenly spread. |
| **Annual bonus** | Cash bonus based on performance (10 – 25% of base, varies by company) | Google: 15 – 30% target. Meta: 10 – 25%. Amazon: minimal. |
| **Signing bonus** | One-time cash payment (sometimes spread over 1 – 2 years) | Negotiable and often the easiest lever to increase. Amazon uses this to bridge the back-loaded RSU gap. |

**How to research:**
- **levels.fyi** — The gold standard. Filter by company, role, level, and location.
- **Blind / TeamBlind** — Anonymous compensation posts with verified employer.
- **Glassdoor** — Useful but less granular than levels.fyi.
- **comp.fyi** — Crowd-sourced compensation data.

**What to prepare:**
- Your **target total compensation** (TC) range at the level you're targeting.
- Your **current compensation** (some companies ask — know how to frame it).
- Your **walk-away number** — the TC below which you'd decline.

**Typical Big Tech TC ranges for AI roles (US, 2025-2026):**

| Level | Google | Meta | Amazon | OpenAI/Anthropic |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **L3 / Junior** | $180 – $250K | $170 – $240K | $150 – $220K | $200 – $280K |
| **L4 / Mid** | $250 – $350K | $240 – $340K | $220 – $310K | $280 – $380K |
| **L5 / Senior** | $350 – $500K | $340 – $480K | $310 – $440K | $380 – $550K |
| **L6 / Staff** | $500 – $750K | $480 – $700K | $440 – $650K | $550 – $800K+ |
| **L7+ / Principal** | $750K – $1M+ | $700K – $1M+ | $650K – $900K+ | $800K – $1.2M+ |

> **Note**: These ranges are approximate and vary by location, team, and negotiation. Use levels.fyi for current data.

### 3. Prepare Your 60-Second Elevator Pitch

When the recruiter says "Tell me about yourself," deliver a **tight, structured pitch** that maps directly to the role.

**Structure (4 parts, 60 seconds total):**

```
Part 1 — Who you are now (10 sec):
"I'm a [title] at [Company], where I [one-sentence summary of what you do]."

Part 2 — Your relevant track record (20 sec):
"Over the past [X] years, I've [2-3 accomplishments relevant to this role].
For example, [one specific, quantified achievement]."

Part 3 — Your technical identity (15 sec):
"My core expertise is in [technical area 1] and [technical area 2],
and I've worked extensively with [tools/frameworks relevant to the job]."

Part 4 — Why THIS role (15 sec):
"I'm particularly excited about [specific team/product] because [genuine reason
tied to your career goals or interests]."
```

**Role-specific pitch examples:**

**AI Engineer:**
> "I'm an AI Engineer at [Company], where I build production LLM applications. Over the past three years, I've shipped a RAG-based customer support system handling 50K queries/day, and built an evaluation framework that reduced hallucination rates by 40%. My core expertise is in LLM integration, prompt engineering, and retrieval systems — I work daily with LangChain, FastAPI, and vector databases. I'm excited about [Company]'s [specific product] because I want to build AI systems at the kind of scale that only [Company] operates at."

**AI Systems Engineer:**
> "I'm an AI Systems Engineer at [Company], where I build and optimize the infrastructure behind our ML platform. I've designed a model serving pipeline that handles 10K inferences/second at p99 latency under 100ms, and migrated our training infrastructure to support distributed fine-tuning across multi-GPU clusters. My core expertise is in ML infrastructure, model serving, and GPU optimization — I work daily with Kubernetes, Triton, and distributed training frameworks. I'm excited about [Company]'s [specific infra/platform] because the scale of the problems here is an order of magnitude beyond what I could tackle anywhere else."

**AI Agentic Engineer:**
> "I'm an AI Engineer specializing in agentic systems at [Company], where I build autonomous AI workflows. I've designed and shipped a multi-agent system that automates [specific process], reducing manual effort by 70%, and built a tool-calling framework that handles 30+ integrations with robust error recovery. My core expertise is in agent architectures, tool orchestration, and LLM-powered automation. I'm excited about [Company]'s work on [specific agent/product] because the complexity of real-world agentic systems at [Company]'s scale is exactly the problem I want to solve."

### 4. Prepare for Level Calibration Questions

Big Tech recruiters are trained to **calibrate your level** during this call. They will ask questions designed to gauge your seniority:

| Question | What They're Really Asking | How to Answer |
|---|---|---|
| "How many years of experience do you have?" | Level calibration baseline | State total YoE + years specifically in AI/ML |
| "What's the size of the team you work with?" | Scope signal | Mention team size AND your role within it |
| "Do you manage anyone?" | IC vs. manager track | Clarify: "I'm on the IC track. I mentor 2 junior engineers but don't formally manage." |
| "Can you give me an example of a project you led?" | Ownership & scope signal | Pick a project that matches your target level's scope |
| "What's the most technically complex thing you've built?" | Depth signal | Choose something that demonstrates the depth expected at your target level |
| "How do you work with cross-functional teams?" | Collaboration scope | L3-4: "I work with..." / L5: "I lead collaboration with..." / L6+: "I drive cross-org initiatives..." |

> **Warning**: If the recruiter calibrates you at a lower level than you're targeting, the entire interview loop will be evaluated against a lower bar — which means lower compensation. **Prepare to clearly articulate why you should be at your target level.**

## During the Call

### The Typical 30-Minute Flow

| Time | Phase | What Happens |
|---|---|---|
| 0 – 2 min | **Intro** | Recruiter introduces themselves and explains the process. |
| 2 – 5 min | **Your pitch** | "Tell me about yourself." Deliver your 60-second pitch. |
| 5 – 12 min | **Role & experience deep-dive** | Recruiter asks about your background, specific experience, and technical skills. |
| 12 – 18 min | **Level calibration** | Questions about scope, leadership, complexity of past work. |
| 18 – 22 min | **Logistics** | Visa, timeline, location, notice period, competing offers. |
| 22 – 26 min | **Compensation** | Salary expectations and current compensation (if asked). |
| 26 – 30 min | **Your questions** | 2 – 3 questions about the role, team, or process. |

### Common Questions and How to Answer Them

**"Tell me about yourself."**
→ Deliver your 60-second elevator pitch exactly as prepared.

**"Why [Company]?"**
→ Be specific. Never say "It's a great company" or "I want to work at a top company."
→ Instead: *"I've been following [Company]'s work on [specific product/research]. The technical challenges around [specific problem] are exactly what I want to spend the next few years working on. I also think [Company]'s approach to [specific thing — open-source, safety, scale, etc.] aligns with how I think about building AI responsibly."*

**"Why this specific role / team?"**
→ Connect your skills to the team's mission: *"This role sits at the intersection of [your strength] and [team's focus]. Specifically, [team]'s work on [specific project] maps directly to my experience building [specific thing]. I'd be contributing from day one while also growing into [area you want to develop]."*

**"Why are you leaving your current role?"**
→ **Never badmouth your current company.** Frame it as moving toward something, not away from something.
→ Template: *"I've learned a lot at [current company] — I [specific achievement]. But I've reached a point where I want to [specific growth goal: scale, impact, depth, team quality], and [target company] is the best place for that."*

**"What's your timeline?"**
→ Be honest. If you have competing offers, say so — it creates urgency: *"I'm in the final stages with [one or two other companies] and expect to have offers within [X weeks]. I'd love to move quickly with [company] because it's my top choice."*
→ If you don't have competing offers, don't lie. Instead: *"I'm actively interviewing and would love to move efficiently. My notice period is [X weeks], so I could start by [date]."*

### Handling the Compensation Question

This is where most candidates lose money. Big Tech recruiters are **trained negotiators**.

**If they ask "What are your salary expectations?"**

**Option A — Deflect and redirect (recommended for early stage):**
> *"I'd prefer to learn more about the role and the full compensation structure before we discuss numbers. I'm confident we can make the numbers work — can you share the range for this level?"*

**Option B — Anchor high with a range (if pushed):**
> *"Based on my research on levels.fyi and conversations with peers, I'd expect the total comp for an [L5/Senior] role at [Company] to be in the $[X] – $[Y] range. But I'm flexible — the right role and team matter more to me than hitting an exact number."*

**Option C — Share current comp strategically (if required):**
> *"My current total compensation is approximately $[X], which includes base, stock, and bonus. I'm looking for a meaningful step up that reflects the increased scope and impact of this role."*

**Rules:**
- Always talk in **total compensation (TC)**, not just base salary.
- Never give a single number — always a range.
- If they ask your current comp and you're in a jurisdiction where that question is illegal (California, New York, etc.), you can decline: *"I'd prefer not to share my current compensation, as I'd like to be evaluated based on the value I bring to this role."*
- Get THEIR range first whenever possible. Big Tech companies have defined bands — the recruiter knows the range.

### Questions to Ask the Recruiter

Ask 2 – 3 of these to show engagement and gather intel:

| Question | Why It Helps |
|---|---|
| "Can you walk me through the interview process and timeline?" | You need to know exactly what to prepare for. |
| "What level is this role mapped to, and what does the team consider the ideal background?" | Confirms level calibration and what the team values most. |
| "How large is the team, and what does the team's tech stack look like?" | Intel for tailoring your answers in later rounds. |
| "What are the company's priorities for this team in the next 6 – 12 months?" | Shows strategic thinking and gives you material for system design and behavioural rounds. |
| "Is there anything specific I should prepare or focus on for the technical rounds?" | Some recruiters will share hints. Always ask. |
| "What does the onsite look like — how many rounds, and what does each cover?" | Maps your prep plan directly to the loop structure. |

## After the Call

### Immediate Actions (Within 2 Hours)

1. **Send a thank-you email** (brief, professional):

```
Subject: Thank you — [Role Title] Interview

Hi [Recruiter Name],

Thank you for taking the time to speak with me today. I'm very excited about the
[Role Title] position on the [Team Name] and look forward to the next steps.

Please don't hesitate to reach out if you need any additional information from me.

Best regards,
[Your Name]
```

2. **Write down everything you learned:**
   - What level are they targeting?
   - What does the loop look like?
   - What did the recruiter emphasize?
   - Any hints about what to prepare?
   - Timeline and next steps.

3. **Adjust your prep plan** based on what you learned. If the recruiter said "we'll have two coding rounds and a system design," weight your preparation accordingly.

### If the Recruiter Goes Silent

Big Tech processes are slow. Don't panic if you don't hear back for 1 – 2 weeks. Follow up professionally:

**After 5 business days:**
> *"Hi [Name], I hope you're doing well. I wanted to follow up on our conversation about the [Role Title] position. I'm still very enthusiastic about the opportunity and would love to learn about the next steps. Please let me know if there's anything else you need from me."*

**After 10 business days:**
> *"Hi [Name], I wanted to touch base one more time regarding the [Role Title] role. I have a couple of other processes moving forward and would love to prioritize [Company] as it remains my top choice. Could you share an update on the timeline?"*

> **Pro tip**: Mentioning competing timelines (honestly) is the single most effective way to accelerate a Big Tech process.

## Common Mistakes That Kill Your Chances

| Mistake | Why It's Fatal | What to Do Instead |
|---|---|---|
| **Rambling for 5+ minutes on "Tell me about yourself"** | Signals poor communication. The recruiter loses focus. | Deliver a tight 60-second pitch. Practice it until it's natural. |
| **Not knowing the company/team** | Signals low interest. Mass-applying vibes. | Spend 45 min on targeted research. Name the specific team and product. |
| **Giving a single salary number** | You anchored low and have no room to negotiate. | Always give a range. Always talk TC, not base. |
| **Badmouthing your current employer** | Instant red flag. Suggests you might do the same to them. | Frame your move as "toward opportunity," not "away from problems." |
| **Being passive or unengaged** | "I'm just exploring options" reads as unserious. | Show genuine enthusiasm for the specific team, not just the company brand. |
| **Underselling your scope** | Get calibrated lower → lower comp, easier bar but lower ceiling. | Explicitly articulate the scope, impact, and complexity of your work at your target level. |
| **Not asking questions** | Recruiter sees you as passive or disengaged. | Prepare 3 – 4 questions. Always ask at least 2. |
| **Lying about competing offers** | Recruiters talk to each other and can verify. | Be honest. If you don't have offers, say you're "actively interviewing." |

## Recruiter Screen Scoring Mental Model

While recruiter screens aren't formally scored like onsite rounds, recruiters make a **pass/no-pass decision** based on:

| Signal | Strong Pass | Borderline | No Pass |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Communication** | Clear, concise, structured | Verbose but coherent | Rambling, disorganized, hard to follow |
| **Technical match** | Experience maps directly to the role | Relevant adjacent experience | Experience doesn't match the JD |
| **Level match** | Scope and impact match the target level | Close but might be half a level off | Clearly under or over-leveled |
| **Enthusiasm** | Specific, informed reasons for wanting the role | Generic "great company" statements | Sounds like mass-applying |
| **Comp alignment** | Range is within the band | Slightly above band (negotiable) | Wildly misaligned |
| **Logistics** | No blockers | Minor complexity (visa processing, etc.) | Major blockers (availability, location) |

> **You need at minimum 4 out of 6 Strong Pass or Borderline to advance.** One "No Pass" on communication or technical match is usually disqualifying.

## Quick Reference: Day-of-Call Checklist

- [ ] Quiet environment with good internet / phone signal.
- [ ] Laptop open with:
  - [ ] The job description.
  - [ ] Your 60-second pitch (notes, not a script to read verbatim).
  - [ ] Company/team research notes.
  - [ ] Your compensation range.
  - [ ] 3 – 4 prepared questions.
- [ ] Notebook for taking notes on what you learn.
- [ ] Water nearby.

- [ ] Smile — it comes through even on phone calls.

## Company-Specific Nuances

| Company | Recruiter Screen Notes |
|---|---|
| **Google** | Recruiter may ask basic technical screening questions. Level calibration is critical — hiring committee uses it. |
| **Meta** | Fast-moving process. Recruiter may try to schedule the full loop within 1 – 2 weeks of the screen. |
| **Amazon** | Expect LP-related questions even from the recruiter ("Tell me about a time you demonstrated ownership"). |
| **Apple** | More secretive. Recruiter may not share much about the team or projects. Focus on your background. |
| **Microsoft** | Recruiter may discuss specific team matching — you might interview for multiple teams in parallel. |
| **OpenAI / Anthropic** | More conversational. May ask about your views on AI safety, alignment, or responsible AI. |
| **NVIDIA** | May ask about your experience with GPU/CUDA/systems-level work early on. |
