# Round 1: Recruiter Screen — How to Pass It Excellently

> **Format**: Phone or video call | **Duration**: 20 – 30 min | **Interviewer**: Recruiter or talent partner

---

## What This Round Actually Is

The recruiter screen is the **gateway**. It looks simple — a friendly 20-minute chat — but roughly **50 – 60% of candidates are eliminated here**. The recruiter is not evaluating your ability to design RAG systems or train models. They are running a **qualification and calibration check**:

1. **Can this person communicate clearly?** (If you can't hold a 20-minute conversation, you can't survive a 5-round loop.)
2. **Do their qualifications match the job?** (Years of experience, relevant skills, right domain.)
3. **Are salary expectations aligned?** (If you want $300K and the band tops at $180K, this ends now.)
4. **Are they genuinely interested?** (Or are they mass-applying and will ghost after round 2?)
5. **Are there logistical blockers?** (Visa, start date, location/remote compatibility.)

> **Key insight**: The recruiter is your *advocate* if you pass this round. They will pitch you internally to the hiring manager. Give them **clear, quotable reasons** to champion you.

---

## Before the Call

### 1. Research the Company (30 – 45 min)

You need enough context to show genuine interest, not enough to write a dissertation.

- **What they do**: One paragraph summary of the product and who it serves.
- **Where AI fits in**: How does AI/ML power their product? (Check the product page, blog posts, or press releases.)
- **Recent news**: Any funding round, product launch, partnership, or acquisition in the last 6 months.
- **The role**: Re-read the JD. Be able to explain why your background matches in 2 – 3 sentences.

### 2. Know Your Numbers

Recruiters will ask about compensation. Being unprepared here is an instant credibility hit.

- **Research the market range** for your role and level using:
  - levels.fyi (best for tech roles)
  - Glassdoor salary data
  - Blind (anonymous compensation posts)
  - LinkedIn salary insights
- **Set your range**: Know your **minimum** (walk-away number) and your **target** (what you'd be genuinely happy with).
- **Include total compensation**, not just base: base salary + equity/stock + bonus + signing bonus.
- **If you're unsure**, it's okay to say: *"I'm flexible and more focused on the right opportunity, but based on my research, I'd expect the total comp to be in the range of $X – $Y. I'd love to learn more about your compensation structure."*

### 3. Prepare Your "Elevator Pitch" (60 Seconds)

When the recruiter says "Tell me about yourself" or "Walk me through your background," deliver this:

**Structure** (4 parts, 60 seconds total):

| Part | What to Say | Duration |
|---|---|---|
| **1. Identity** | Your current role/title and focus area | 1 sentence |
| **2. Relevant experience** | The 1 – 2 most relevant things you've done | 2 – 3 sentences |
| **3. Why this company** | What specifically attracted you to this role | 1 – 2 sentences |
| **4. What you bring** | The key value you'd add | 1 sentence |

**Example:**
> "I'm an AI engineer with three years of experience building LLM-powered products, most recently focused on RAG systems and conversational agents. At my current company, I designed and shipped a document Q&A pipeline that handles over 50,000 documents and reduced customer support load by 30%. I came across your role because I've been using your product and I'm really interested in the retrieval challenges you're solving for the legal space. I think my hands-on experience with production RAG systems and agent design would let me contribute quickly."

### 4. Prepare Your "Why This Company" Answer

This is the question that separates prepared candidates from spray-and-prayers. Have 2 – 3 **specific** reasons ready:

- **Product or mission connection**: "I've been using your product and I'm impressed by how you handle X."
- **Technical challenge alignment**: "The JD mentions scaling retrieval pipelines, which is exactly what I've been doing."
- **Team or culture**: "I read [blog post / talk / podcast] by your Head of AI and their approach to evaluation really resonates with how I think."

### 5. Prepare Questions to Ask the Recruiter

Have 2 – 3 questions ready. Good ones for this round:

- "Can you walk me through what the rest of the interview process looks like?"
- "How is the AI/ML team structured? How many people would I be working with?"
- "What's the timeline for filling this role?"
- "Is the role remote, hybrid, or on-site?"
- "What does the compensation structure look like? (base, equity, bonus)"

## During the Call

### Common Questions + How to Handle Them

#### "Tell me about yourself / Walk me through your background."
→ Use your 60-second elevator pitch from above. Do NOT recite your entire resume.

#### "Why are you interested in this role / company?"
→ Use your prepared "Why This Company" answer. Be specific — name the product, a recent development, or a technical challenge from the JD.

#### "What are you looking for in your next role?"
→ Frame it around what mid-size companies offer:
> "I'm looking for a role where I can go deep on [specific domain], work with a strong engineering team, and see my work directly impact the product. I want more structure than a tiny startup but more ownership and speed than a large corporation."

#### "Walk me through your experience with [specific technology from JD]."
→ Give a **concrete, concise example**:
> "I've been working with LangGraph for the past 8 months. My most recent project was a multi-step agent that automates data extraction from contracts — it uses tool calling to query a database, validate results, and generate structured reports. It's running in production and processes about 500 contracts a week."

Don't oversell. If your experience is limited, be honest and show eagerness:
> "I've built a couple of prototypes with it but haven't used it in production yet. That said, I've been deep in the docs and the patterns are very similar to what I've done with [related tool]."

#### "What are your salary expectations?"
→ This is the most sensitive question. Options:

**Option A — Give a range (recommended):**
> "Based on my research and experience, I'd expect total compensation in the range of $150K – $180K, depending on the equity and bonus structure. But I'm flexible — I'd love to understand how your comp bands work."

**Option B — Deflect (if you need more info):**
> "I don't have a hard number yet because I'd like to understand the full package — base, equity, bonus, and benefits. Could you share the range for this role so I can see if we're aligned?"

**Option C — If they push:**
> "My current total comp is around $X, and I'd expect a meaningful step up for this move. What's the range you have budgeted?"

**What to avoid:**
- ❌ Giving a number way below market (you'll get lowballed).
- ❌ Refusing to engage ("I don't discuss salary until I have an offer") — this is fine at Big Tech but can be off-putting at mid-size.
- ❌ Lying about current compensation.

#### "What's your timeline? Are you interviewing elsewhere?"
→ Be honest but create gentle urgency:
> "I'm actively interviewing with a few companies, so my timeline is the next 2 – 3 weeks ideally. But this role is a top priority for me — I'd make time for your process."

#### "Are you open to [on-site / hybrid / relocation]?"
→ Be straightforward. If there's a dealbreaker, surface it now:
> "I'm based in [city] and prefer remote, but I'm open to hybrid if the team is concentrated in [their city]. Could you tell me how the AI team typically works?"

#### "Do you have questions for me?"
→ Ask your prepared questions. **Always ask at least 2.** Asking zero questions signals low interest.

## Body Language & Communication Tips (Even for Phone Calls)

| Do | Don't |
|---|---|
| Smile when speaking — it changes your vocal tone | Sound flat or bored |
| Speak at a moderate pace — slightly slower than normal | Rush through answers nervously |
| Use the interviewer's name once or twice | Over-use their name (feels forced) |
| Take a 1-second pause before answering (shows you're thinking) | Interrupt or start talking before they finish |
| Keep answers to 60 – 90 seconds unless asked to elaborate | Monologue for 3+ minutes |
| Have your notes nearby for reference | Read from a script word-for-word (they can tell) |
| Use a quiet room with good connection | Take the call from a noisy cafe or while driving |

## After the Call

### Within 1 Hour
- **Send a short thank-you email** (2 – 3 sentences):
  > "Hi [recruiter name], thanks for taking the time to chat today. I'm excited about the role and the work the AI team is doing on [specific thing]. Looking forward to the next steps!"
- This is not universal practice, but it costs you 2 minutes and can tip the scale if you're on the edge of advancing.

### Within 24 Hours
- **Write down everything the recruiter told you** about the process, team, and role. You'll need this for later rounds:
  - Who will interview you next?
  - What format will the technical round be?
  - What does the team work on?
  - Any hints about what they're looking for?

## Common Mistakes That Kill Candidates at This Stage

| Mistake | Why It's Fatal |
|---|---|
| **Not researching the company** | Recruiter asks "Why us?" and you have no answer. Instant pass. |
| **Rambling answers** | 3-minute answers to simple questions signal poor communication. |
| **Trashing your current/past employer** | Red flag for attitude and professionalism. |
| **Being evasive about compensation** | Creates friction and wastes everyone's time. |
| **Having no questions** | Signals you don't care or aren't serious. |
| **Being late or having technical issues** | Shows a lack of preparation and respect for their time. |
| **Overselling or exaggerating** | Recruiters talk to dozens of candidates — they can smell BS. |
| **Treating the recruiter as "less important"** | The recruiter decides if you advance. They are the first decision-maker. |

## What Passing Looks Like

After a successful recruiter screen, the recruiter will:
1. Confirm next steps on the call or via email within 1 – 3 business days.
2. Schedule you for **Round 2 (Hiring Manager call)**.
3. Possibly share prep materials, interview format details, or the names of your interviewers.

> **If you don't hear back in 5 business days**, send a polite follow-up: *"Hi [name], just checking in on next steps for the [role title] position. I'm still very interested and happy to provide any additional information you need."*

## Key Takeaway

> The recruiter screen is a **gate, not a test**. You don't need to be brilliant — you need to be **clear, prepared, and genuine**. Show that you know what the company does, why you want to be there, and that your skills match. Make it easy for the recruiter to say "yes, advance this person."
